The Basics
What Are Peptides?

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — the same building blocks that make up proteins. Your body naturally produces thousands of peptides that act as signaling molecules, telling cells to do things like repair tissue, release hormones, regulate inflammation, and control appetite.

The peptides being discussed in wellness circles are typically synthetic versions of naturally occurring peptides, designed to mimic or enhance your body's own signaling. Think of them as highly targeted biological messengers.

GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro) — are themselves peptide-based drugs, which is why there is so much overlap between the GLP-1 world and the broader peptide conversation.

The GLP-1 + Peptide Connection

If you are on a GLP-1 medication, you are already using a peptide-based drug. Semaglutide and tirzepatide work by mimicking naturally occurring GLP-1 and GIP hormones — peptides your gut produces after eating.

Understanding this helps contextualize why so many GLP-1 users become interested in the broader peptide space. The mechanisms are similar — targeted biological signaling — and the goals often overlap: body composition, metabolic health, and longevity.

From my esthetics perspective, the most practical overlap is with tissue repair and skin peptides. Post-GLP-1 skin laxity is real, and peptide-based topical treatments combined with professional treatments are part of a comprehensive approach to body confidence after significant weight loss.

Expanded Peptide Library
Individual Peptide Profiles

Each profile covers what the peptide is, proposed mechanisms, what people claim, the science reality, and WADA status. Educational only — no recommendations.

BPC-157
Body Protection Compound — a synthetic peptide derived from a protein found in human gastric juice.
Proposed Mechanism: Promotes angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation), modulates nitric oxide, and supports tendon/ligament repair signaling.
What People Claim: Accelerated healing of tendons, ligaments, gut lining, and muscle tears. Some report rapid recovery from injury.
Science Reality: Promising animal studies. Very limited human clinical data. Most evidence is preclinical (rats, mice). No FDA approval.
Trainer Talking Points: If a client asks, acknowledge interest, explain the research gap, and emphasize that NASM CES corrective exercise is the evidence-based approach for injury recovery.
WADA: Not explicitly listed but check current list
TB-500
Synthetic version of Thymosin Beta-4 — a naturally occurring peptide involved in cell migration and tissue repair.
Proposed Mechanism: Promotes cell migration to injury sites, reduces inflammation, supports wound healing and tissue remodeling.
What People Claim: Enhanced recovery from muscle tears, tendon injuries, and general inflammation. Often paired with BPC-157.
Science Reality: Animal studies show wound healing benefits. Limited human trials. Not FDA approved for any indication.
Trainer Talking Points: Recovery is multifactorial — sleep, nutrition, and progressive loading matter more than any single compound.
WADA: Banned in competition
Ipamorelin
A selective growth hormone secretagogue that stimulates the pituitary gland to release growth hormone.
Proposed Mechanism: Mimics ghrelin at the GH secretagogue receptor, triggering GH release without significantly affecting cortisol or prolactin.
What People Claim: Improved body composition, better sleep, enhanced recovery, anti-aging effects, increased lean mass.
Science Reality: Some clinical data supports GH release. Long-term safety data is limited. Available through some TRT/anti-aging clinics with prescription.
Trainer Talking Points: GH optimization starts with sleep quality, resistance training, and nutrition. Compounds are supplementary at best.
WADA: Banned — growth hormone releasing peptide
CJC-1295
A synthetic analog of growth hormone releasing hormone (GHRH) with extended half-life via DAC modification.
Proposed Mechanism: Stimulates sustained GH release from the pituitary. Often combined with Ipamorelin for synergistic effect.
What People Claim: Body fat reduction, increased lean mass, improved recovery, deeper sleep, and anti-aging benefits.
Science Reality: Clinical studies show measurable GH elevation. Safety profile less established than approved GH therapies. Not FDA approved.
Trainer Talking Points: Clients should know that progressive overload and adequate protein (0.7-1g/lb) naturally optimize GH response.
WADA: Banned — GHRH analog
Sermorelin
A synthetic version of the first 29 amino acids of GHRH. One of the earliest GH-releasing peptides used clinically.
Proposed Mechanism: Stimulates natural GH production and release from the pituitary in a pulsatile pattern.
What People Claim: Improved sleep, body composition, energy levels, and skin quality. Considered milder than direct GH therapy.
Science Reality: Was FDA-approved for pediatric GH deficiency (since discontinued for commercial reasons). Reasonable safety data exists. Available through anti-aging clinics.
Trainer Talking Points: Focus on lifestyle factors that naturally support GH: deep sleep, high-intensity resistance training, and reduced sugar intake.
WADA: Banned — GHRH analog
AOD-9604
Anti-Obesity Drug 9604 — a modified fragment of human growth hormone (amino acids 177-191).
Proposed Mechanism: Stimulates lipolysis (fat breakdown) and inhibits lipogenesis (fat formation) without affecting IGF-1 or insulin.
What People Claim: Targeted fat loss without the side effects of full GH therapy. Marketed for body composition improvement.
Science Reality: Phase 2 clinical trials showed modest weight loss effects. Did not proceed to Phase 3. Limited published data. Not FDA approved.
Trainer Talking Points: Caloric deficit through nutrition and progressive training remains the gold standard for fat loss. No peptide replaces fundamentals.
WADA: Banned
Hexarelin
A synthetic growth hormone releasing peptide (GHRP) — one of the strongest GH secretagogues available.
Proposed Mechanism: Stimulates GH release via the ghrelin receptor. Also studied for cardioprotective effects.
What People Claim: Significant GH elevation, improved body composition, cardiac benefits, and enhanced recovery.
Science Reality: Studies confirm strong GH release but also show rapid desensitization (tolerance develops). Cardiac benefits shown in animal models.
Trainer Talking Points: Tolerance issues make this impractical for long-term use. Natural GH optimization through training is sustainable.
WADA: Banned — GHRP
GHRP-2
Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide-2 — a synthetic agonist of the ghrelin receptor.
Proposed Mechanism: Stimulates GH release and also increases appetite via ghrelin pathway activation.
What People Claim: Strong GH elevation, improved sleep, muscle recovery, and increased appetite (beneficial for underweight clients).
Science Reality: Well-studied in clinical settings. Does increase GH and cortisol. Appetite stimulation can be a significant side effect.
Trainer Talking Points: The appetite increase can work against clients in a fat loss phase. For GLP-1 users, this creates a direct conflict.
WADA: Banned — GHRP
GHRP-6
Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide-6 — another ghrelin receptor agonist with stronger appetite-stimulating effects.
Proposed Mechanism: Similar to GHRP-2 but with more pronounced hunger signaling. Stimulates GH release and gastric motility.
What People Claim: GH elevation, recovery enhancement, and significant appetite increase. Sometimes used to help underweight individuals gain weight.
Science Reality: Confirmed GH release in studies. Strong appetite stimulation is the primary distinguishing feature. Not ideal for weight management goals.
Trainer Talking Points: Directly contradicts GLP-1 goals. Appetite stimulation in a weight loss context is counterproductive.
WADA: Banned — GHRP
PT-141
Bremelanotide — a melanocortin receptor agonist. FDA-approved as Vyleesi for hypoactive sexual desire disorder in premenopausal women.
Proposed Mechanism: Acts on melanocortin-4 receptors in the brain to influence sexual arousal pathways. Does not work through vascular mechanisms.
What People Claim: Enhanced sexual desire and function for both men and women. Sometimes marketed in wellness/anti-aging clinics.
Science Reality: FDA-approved (as Vyleesi) for a specific indication. Side effects include nausea and flushing. Off-label use for men is not FDA approved.
Trainer Talking Points: Sexual wellness is part of overall health. Exercise itself is one of the best evidence-based approaches to improving sexual function.
WADA: Check current list — melanocortin agonists may be monitored
Epithalon
A synthetic tetrapeptide studied for its potential effects on telomerase activation and longevity.
Proposed Mechanism: Proposed to activate telomerase, the enzyme that maintains telomere length — associated with cellular aging.
What People Claim: Anti-aging at the cellular level, improved sleep quality, enhanced immune function, and longevity benefits.
Science Reality: Based primarily on Russian research by Dr. Vladimir Khavinson. Limited Western peer-reviewed data. Intriguing hypothesis but insufficient clinical evidence.
Trainer Talking Points: Longevity science points to exercise, sleep, and nutrition as the most evidence-based interventions. Telomere research is early-stage.
WADA: Not explicitly listed — verify current list
LL-37
A human cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide — part of the innate immune system.
Proposed Mechanism: Disrupts microbial membranes, modulates immune response, and promotes wound healing. Naturally produced by immune cells.
What People Claim: Enhanced immune defense, improved wound healing, anti-biofilm activity, and potential anti-cancer properties.
Science Reality: Well-characterized in immunology research. Therapeutic applications are in early clinical stages. Synthetic versions available through research suppliers.
Trainer Talking Points: Immune health is best supported by adequate sleep, vitamin D, zinc, exercise, and stress management. Peptide immune modulators are research-stage.
WADA: Not explicitly listed — verify current list
✨ Topical Peptides in Skincare — Debora's Esthetics Angle

As a licensed esthetician, this is where peptides and my professional practice intersect directly. Topical peptides are among the most exciting ingredients in modern esthetics — backed by real research and used in professional-grade skincare.

Copper Peptides (GHK-Cu): One of the best-studied topical peptides. GHK-Cu promotes collagen synthesis, reduces inflammation, and supports wound healing. I use copper peptide serums in my practice for clients experiencing post-weight-loss skin changes and post-procedure recovery. Studies in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology confirm its collagen-stimulating properties.

Argireline (Acetyl Hexapeptide-3): Often called "topical Botox" — it works by inhibiting neurotransmitter release at the neuromuscular junction, reducing the appearance of expression lines. It does not replace Botox but is a reasonable daily maintenance ingredient.

Matrixyl (Palmitoyl Pentapeptide-4): Stimulates collagen I and III production. One of the most evidence-backed anti-aging peptides in skincare. Multiple clinical trials demonstrate wrinkle reduction with consistent use.

Palmitoyl Tripeptide-1 and Tetrapeptide-7: Work synergistically to reduce inflammation and stimulate extracellular matrix repair. Found in many professional-grade anti-aging formulations.

My Protocol for Post-Weight-Loss Skin: Combine topical copper peptides + collagen supplementation (15-20g hydrolyzed collagen daily) + resistance training (builds the underlying muscle structure) + professional treatments (laser, radiofrequency, microneedling) + hydration and patience. Transformation is a process, not an event.
