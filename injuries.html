Injury Education
and Workarounds

You do not stop training. You train smarter. Every injury card below includes what to avoid, what still works, and the NASM CES corrective sequence. This is how we keep moving forward.

Important — Educational Content Only

This page is for educational purposes only. Always consult a physician or physical therapist before training with an injury. Dr. Michael Lovato is not a licensed physician.

If you experience numbness, radiating pain, loss of bowel or bladder control, or sudden severe pain, stop all activity and seek immediate medical attention.

🦵 Knee Injuries
Meniscus tears, ACL sprains, patellofemoral syndrome (runner's knee). Among the most common training injuries, especially in squatters and runners.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Deep squats below parallel with heavy load
  • High-impact jumping and plyometrics
  • Heavy leg extensions (open chain knee stress)
  • Running on hard surfaces
Movements That Still Work
  • Leg press with limited range of motion
  • Wall sits (isometric hold)
  • Hip bridges and glute work
  • Upper body training (no restrictions)
  • Pool exercises and cycling (low impact)
NASM CES Corrective Sequence
  • Inhibit: Foam roll quads, IT band, and calves
  • Lengthen: Stretch hip flexors and calves
  • Activate: VMO activation with terminal knee extensions
  • Integrate: Single-leg balance progressions, step-ups
When to See a Professional
  • Knee locks or gives way during walking
  • Swelling that does not resolve in 48 hours
  • Sharp pain with any weight-bearing activity
💪 Shoulder Injuries
Rotator cuff tears/strains, impingement syndrome, labral tears. Extremely common in lifters, especially those who bench press and overhead press frequently.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Overhead press with pain
  • Behind-the-neck movements
  • Heavy chest flyes at full range
  • Upright rows
Movements That Still Work
  • Landmine press (shoulder-friendly angle)
  • Floor press (limited ROM protects shoulder)
  • Band pull-aparts and face pulls
  • Light lateral raises (controlled)
  • All lower body training
NASM CES Corrective Sequence
  • Inhibit: Foam roll lats, pecs, and upper traps
  • Lengthen: Doorway chest stretch, cross-body shoulder stretch
  • Activate: External rotation with band, prone Y-T-W raises
  • Integrate: Cable rows with retraction focus, push-up plus
Dr. Lovato Personal Note: I train with a torn shoulder every week. The key is finding the angles that do not cause pain and building strength there. Landmine press and floor press became my primary pressing movements. You adapt. You overcome. You never stop.
🔙 Lower Back Pain
Disc bulges, muscle strains, facet joint irritation. The most common pain complaint in the general population and among lifters.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Heavy deadlifts from the floor
  • Loaded spinal flexion (good mornings with heavy load)
  • Sit-ups and crunches
  • Seated cable rows with excessive forward lean
Movements That Still Work
  • McGill Big 3: Curl-up, Side Plank, Bird Dog
  • Hip hinge with light load (kettlebell deadlift)
  • Dead bugs and pallof press
  • Walking and swimming
  • Upper body pressing and pulling (supported)
NASM CES Corrective Sequence
  • Inhibit: Foam roll hip flexors, piriformis, TFL
  • Lengthen: Hip flexor stretch (half-kneeling), hamstring stretch
  • Activate: Glute bridges, bird dogs, dead bugs
  • Integrate: Hip hinge pattern with dowel, farmer carries
When to See a Professional
  • Numbness or tingling in legs
  • Radiating pain below the knee
  • Loss of bowel or bladder control (emergency)
🦶 Plantar Fasciitis
Inflammation of the plantar fascia — the thick band of tissue on the bottom of the foot. Sharp heel pain, especially first thing in the morning.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Running on hard surfaces
  • Plyometrics and box jumps
  • Prolonged standing without support
  • Walking barefoot on hard floors
Movements That Still Work
  • Cycling and swimming (no foot impact)
  • Seated exercises (leg press, seated row)
  • Controlled calf raises (eccentric loading)
  • Upper body training (no restrictions)
Dr. Lovato Personal Note: I dealt with plantar fasciitis for over a year. Frozen water bottle rolls, night splints, and supportive shoes made the biggest difference. I switched to cycling for cardio and kept lifting. It healed — but it took patience. Do not rush it.
🏃 Hip Flexor and IT Band
Common in runners and cyclists. Hip flexor strains and IT band syndrome cause pain in the front of the hip and outside of the knee respectively.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Deep lunges with poor form
  • Excessive running volume
  • Heavy hip flexion under load
Movements That Still Work
  • Glute bridges and hip thrusts
  • Clamshells and lateral band walks
  • Side-lying leg raises
  • Upper body and core training
NASM CES Corrective Sequence
  • Inhibit: Foam roll TFL, IT band, and hip flexors
  • Lengthen: Kneeling hip flexor stretch, pigeon stretch
  • Activate: Glute medius with clamshells, lateral band walks
  • Integrate: Lateral movements, single-leg balance
💪 Elbow Tendinopathy
Tennis elbow (lateral) and golfer's elbow (medial). Common in grip athletes, climbers, and those who do heavy pulling movements.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Heavy curls and reverse curls
  • Pull-ups with pain
  • Wrist extensions under load
Movements That Still Work
  • Light wrist curls (eccentric loading protocol)
  • Neutral grip movements (hammer curls, neutral pull-downs)
  • Lower body training and core work
  • Pressing movements (typically pain-free)
🦶 Ankle Sprains and Instability
Lateral ankle sprains are the most common sports injury. Chronic instability develops when ligaments do not fully heal or proprioception is not restored.
Movements to Avoid During Flare-Up
  • Lateral cutting without support
  • Heavy single-leg work initially
  • Running on uneven surfaces
Movements That Still Work
  • Seated exercises (full upper body)
  • BOSU balance progressions (as tolerated)
  • Ankle alphabet exercises for mobility
  • Resistance band ankle strengthening (4-way)
General Principles

Train around injuries, not through them. There is always something you can do. An injured shoulder does not mean you stop training legs. An injured knee does not mean you stop training upper body. Adapt. Find what works. Keep moving.

Acute vs Chronic — know the difference. Acute injuries (sudden onset, swelling, sharp pain) need rest and medical evaluation. Chronic conditions (ongoing, dull ache, movement-dependent) often benefit from smart training and corrective exercise.

Ice vs Heat. Acute injuries (first 48-72 hours): ice to reduce swelling. Chronic conditions: heat before activity to increase blood flow and mobility. When in doubt, ice after activity if there is swelling.

RICE vs PEACE and LOVE. The newer evidence-based protocol replaces RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) with PEACE (Protect, Elevate, Avoid anti-inflammatories initially, Compress, Educate) for the first few days, followed by LOVE (Load gradually, Optimism, Vascularization through movement, Exercise progressively).

Role of physical therapy. A good PT is one of the best investments you can make. They assess your specific injury, create a progressive rehabilitation plan, and help you return to full training safely. FORGEIQ complements PT — it does not replace it.

Final Reminder

This page is educational only. Every injury is different. What works for one person may not work for another. Always get a proper diagnosis from a qualified healthcare provider before training with an injury.

If something hurts during exercise — stop. Pain is information. Listen to it.
