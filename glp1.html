The Basics
What Are GLP-1 Medications?

GLP-1 receptor agonists are a class of medications originally developed for type 2 diabetes that have shown remarkable effectiveness for weight management. They work by mimicking a natural hormone (glucagon-like peptide-1) that your gut produces after eating.

How they work: GLP-1 medications slow gastric emptying, reduce appetite signaling in the brain, improve insulin sensitivity, and create a sense of fullness. The result is reduced caloric intake and, for many people, significant weight loss.

Common GLP-1 medications:

  • Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) — Weekly injection. Ozempic is approved for diabetes, Wegovy for weight management.
  • Tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) — Weekly injection. Dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist. Mounjaro for diabetes, Zepbound for weight management.
  • Liraglutide (Saxenda) — Daily injection. One of the earlier GLP-1 medications approved for weight management.
  • Oral Semaglutide (Rybelsus) — Daily pill form. Currently approved for diabetes.
Critical Priority
Why Muscle Preservation is Non-Negotiable

Up to 40% of weight lost on GLP-1 medications can be lean muscle mass — not just fat. This is one of the most important facts every GLP-1 user needs to understand.

Muscle loss leads to reduced metabolic rate, decreased functional strength, increased injury risk, and a "soft" appearance even at lower body weight. The solution is not to avoid the medication — it is to pair it with the right training and nutrition protocol.

  • Resistance training 3-4 times per week is non-negotiable on GLP-1
  • Protein intake: 1-1.2g per pound of bodyweight daily
  • Progressive overload — gradually increase weight, reps, or sets over time
  • NASM Phase 1-2 programming is ideal for most GLP-1 users starting out
  • Never skip protein on training days — even if appetite is suppressed
Debora's Specialty — Skin Laxity

When weight loss happens fast — especially on GLP-1 medications — skin does not always keep up. This is called skin laxity, and it is one of the most common concerns I hear from clients who have lost 50, 80, even 100+ pounds.

Skin laxity is not a failure. It is a natural response to rapid changes in body composition. And there are real, evidence-based approaches to address it:

  • Collagen supplementation: 15-20g hydrolyzed collagen daily supports skin elasticity from the inside
  • Hydration: 80-100oz of water daily is the foundation of skin health
  • Topical peptides: GHK-Cu (copper peptides) stimulate collagen synthesis when applied topically
  • Professional treatments: Laser therapy, radiofrequency, and microneedling can stimulate collagen remodeling
  • Resistance training: Building the underlying muscle structure fills out loose skin naturally
  • Patience and consistency: Skin remodeling takes 6-12+ months. This is a marathon, not a sprint.
Common Concern
Hair Loss on GLP-1 — Telogen Effluvium

Some GLP-1 users experience temporary hair thinning or shedding. This is typically telogen effluvium — a condition where rapid weight loss or nutritional changes cause hair follicles to enter a resting phase prematurely.

What to know: This is almost always temporary. It is not caused by the medication directly, but by the metabolic stress of rapid weight change and potential nutritional deficiencies.

Debora's wellness tips:

  • Ensure adequate protein intake — hair is made of protein (keratin)
  • Supplement biotin (B7) — 2,500-5,000mcg daily
  • Check zinc levels with your doctor — deficiency is common during rapid weight loss
  • Use gentle hair care — avoid tight styles, excessive heat, harsh chemicals
  • Be patient — regrowth typically begins 3-6 months after the trigger stabilizes
  • Talk to your doctor about bloodwork: ferritin, thyroid, vitamin D, and zinc
NASM CNC Protocol
Nutrition Priorities for GLP-1 Users

On GLP-1 medications, appetite is suppressed — which means every meal counts more. Nutrient density is non-negotiable when you are eating less overall.

  • Protein first — always. 1-1.2g per pound of bodyweight. This is the single most important nutritional priority.
  • Electrolytes daily. GLP-1 medications can increase urination and reduce electrolyte intake. Supplement sodium, potassium, and magnesium.
  • B12 supplementation. GLP-1 medications can impair B12 absorption. Ask your doctor about monitoring levels.
  • Vitamin D. Critical for bone health, immune function, and mood — especially during caloric restriction.
  • Small, frequent meals. 4-5 smaller meals help manage nausea and ensure consistent protein intake throughout the day.
  • Hydration: 80-100oz per day. Water supports every metabolic process and helps manage GLP-1 side effects.
  • Collagen supplementation. 15-20g daily supports skin, joint, and connective tissue health during rapid weight loss.
Training Protocol
Exercise Protocol for GLP-1 Users

The goal is clear: preserve muscle, build strength, and support your transformation — not punish your body while it is already under metabolic stress.

  • NASM Phase 1-2 recommended. Start with Stabilization Endurance (Phase 1), progress to Strength Endurance (Phase 2) when ready.
  • Resistance training is priority over cardio. 3-4 sessions per week. Muscle preservation requires progressive resistance.
  • Start with stabilization endurance. 12-20 reps, 1-3 sets, controlled tempo. Build the neuromuscular foundation first.
  • Progressive overload when ready. Gradually increase weight or reps — never sacrifice form for load.
  • Listen to your body on medication days. Some users experience fatigue or nausea after injection. Adjust intensity accordingly.
  • Include core stability work. Planks, dead bugs, bird dogs — core strength supports everything.
  • Light cardio as supplement, not primary. Walking, cycling, swimming — 20-30 minutes as recovery or cardiovascular health, not as the main fat loss strategy.
Empowerment Questions
What to Ask Your Doctor

You are your own best advocate. These questions help you have an informed, productive conversation with your prescribing physician.

  1. What GLP-1 medication is right for my specific situation and health history?
  2. How should I adjust my exercise routine while on this medication?
  3. What bloodwork should I monitor regularly (B12, vitamin D, thyroid, metabolic panel)?
  4. How often should we reassess my dosage based on my progress and side effects?
  5. What are the signs I should contact you immediately?
  6. Should I supplement B12, vitamin D, electrolytes, or other micronutrients?
  7. What is the plan for eventually transitioning off the medication, if that is the goal?
